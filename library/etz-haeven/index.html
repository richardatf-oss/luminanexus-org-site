Etz HaEven — The Stone Tree

A dark, luminous novel grown from a single piece of petrified wood found by your son Matthew—stone as frozen light, memory, and covenant. The Tree of Stone stands where time turns into mineral and voices echo inside the grain.

This page holds the vision and structure for the Stone Tree project. As chapters are completed, it can link to PDFs, print editions, or interactive excerpts.

אֶבֶן

Stone as a seed of memory: every fossil, a buried story.

The stone that became a Tree

The Stone Tree begins with a real stone: a piece of petrified wood found by your son Matthew. Something about its ridges, weight, and quiet age refused to leave the imagination. It felt less like an object and more like a witness—an ancient trunk that had traded sap for quartz and stood there, waiting to be read.

Etz HaEven asks: what happens when a life, a family, or even a whole world “turns to stone”? Is it dead—and therefore beyond change—or has it simply moved into a slower language of transformation?

The novel follows a protagonist who discovers a Stone Tree and begins to hear stories in its rings. Each ring holds a different lifetime of failure, teshuvah, addiction, love, exile, and return. The style leans toward a gothic, Poe-tinged voice—shadowed, dense, and musical—while still rooted in hope.

Underneath the prose is a field-theory of memory: the idea that nothing is truly lost, only compressed. Stone is what happens when light refuses to let go of a story.

What lives in the Stone Tree

Themes and questions that run like mineral veins through Etz HaEven.

Time as mineral

Fossils and petrified wood as condensed years. How many choices, prayers, and silences does it take to become stone? Can a heart that feels petrified ever become a tree again?

Fathers & sons

The book quietly honors Matthew, and by extension every child who brings home a strange rock that turns out to be a gate. What do parents plant in their children, and what do children uncover in their parents?

Sin, shadow, and teshuvah

The Stone Tree holds stories of addiction, shame, and the long walk back. Not as spectacle, but as geology: strata of choice and consequence that can still be walked, layer by layer.

Kabbalah of stone

Even as a symbol of malchut and heaviness; the Luchot as divine writing sunk into sapphire; the “heart of stone” that prophets promise will become a “heart of flesh.” Etz HaEven asks what it means for letters to live inside rock.

Field & fossil

Quantum Etz Chaim imagery runs beneath the surface: the same living Field that moves in waving branches also condenses into stone. Nothing is separate; it’s all one Tree moving through different speeds of time.

Style & atmosphere

A hybrid of literary, mystical, and gothic tones: think “Poe walks through a desert wash with a piece of fossilized wood and a book of midrash.” The Stone Tree is meant to be immersive, heavy, and strangely consoling.

Sample from Chapter Aleph

A short taste of the tone and atmosphere of Etz HaEven. This can grow or be swapped as the manuscript evolves.

Long, parallel ridges—straight as memory, tight as a whisper frozen in place— ran the length of the stone. What had once been sap and bark and branch now lay in his palm as a cooled flame, a quiet geometry of years pressed too close together to name.

He turned it once, twice, as one might turn a question over on the tongue. In the angled late-afternoon light of the wash, the fossil did not simply shine; it seemed to remember. There, in the fine crosshatch of mineral and grain, he felt the faintest pressure of other lives: nights that would not end, days bled out on motel carpets, the slow petrifying of a heart that had once sworn it would never grow hard.

If he listened long enough, he was certain the stone would speak. Not in thunder, not in prophecy, but in the dry, patient cadence of rings stacked upon rings: I was wood once. I carried water. I bent in the wind. What you call “stone” is only what happens when a story refuses to die.

Book structure & progress

A living outline for how Etz HaEven grows on the page.

Dedication

Dedicated to Matthew Ridge, whose stone began the Tree. The dedication page can echo the real object: ridges, weight, and the sense of an ordinary day that opened into eternity.

Chapters as rings

Each chapter is a ring of the Stone Tree, focused on a different era or lifetime. As the reader moves inward, the rings get older, stranger, and closer to the seed of the Tree.

Interludes: voices in the grain

Short interludes—almost like prose poems—where the stone itself speaks. These can carry the heaviest mystical material without breaking the narrative thread.

KDP / print-ready goal

Target: a full novel in print-friendly format (6×9), with front matter, chapter sigils, and a small notes section tying key images back to Tanakh, midrash, and Kabbalah.

Current status
