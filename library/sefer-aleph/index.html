Sefer Aleph — Etz HaReishit (The Tree of Origin)

Aleph as Field, Tilt, and Bridge: a modern walk through the Ari, quantum fields, and the first shimmering of existence from Ein Sof into the Tree of Life.

Citations like Genesis 1:1, Berakhot 2a, Sefer Yetzirah 1:1, and Zohar 1:15a on this page can open directly via Sefaria.

א

The silent letter that contains the whole Tree in potential.

Preface — Before the Tree

A short excerpt from the opening of Sefer Aleph. You can paste the full preface text here as it grows.

In the beginning of this book, there is no tree. No branches, no leaves glistening with dew, no roots tunneling into dark soil. There is only One: הָאֵין סוֹף, the endless, the field before every field. The Torah begins, “In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth” (Genesis 1:1), but before even that first pasuk, the sages speak of Ein Sof—an infinity too simple to be pictured.

Modern physics stumbles toward this in its own language, speaking of unified fields and vacuum states. Kabbalah speaks of Ein Sof as a life so whole that “inside” and “outside” have not yet been imagined. Sefer Yetzirah 1:1 hints that the world is engraved through “thirty-two paths of wisdom,” ten sefirot and twenty-two letters; this Sefer asks what happens if we read those paths alongside the language of quantum fields.

This Sefer is not a physics textbook and not a technical kabbalistic manual. It is a guided walk through the hunch that both are circling: that reality is one living Field which knows how to appear as many.

Chapter Aleph — The First Shimmer of Existence

Aleph as the first almost-sound: the space that lets words be spoken. The chapter unfolds Aleph in three faces.

Aleph as Field

Aleph as the white fire behind the letters: Ein Sof as an unbroken, ever-present field in which all phenomena arise. Classical commentators on Genesis 1:1 and Zohar 1:15a wrestle with what it means for creation to emerge from such hidden fullness. In parallel, quantum field theory describes a “vacuum” that is never truly empty.

Aleph as Tilt

The first inclination within the Field: tzimtzum as a contemplative picture of symmetry breaking, a subtle leaning that allows distinction without severance. Just as the field chooses a particular configuration, so the liturgy in Berakhot 2a imagines the Shema as the moment a person leans the heart toward the oneness of God.

Aleph as Bridge

The written form of Aleph as upper point, lower point, and diagonal: keter and malchut in conversation, direct and returning light, the way the Infinite and the finite speak through each other in every moment. As the Mekubalim read verses like Deuteronomy 4:39 and the Shema in Deuteronomy 6:4, the letter Aleph becomes a diagram of heaven and earth held together.

Excerpt from Chapter Aleph

Imagine absolute darkness—not the darkness of a room where a switch could be flipped, but a darkness in which “light” and “dark” have never yet been conceived. No up, no down, no before or after. Only a fullness so complete that there is no edge to mark it. If such a fullness could have an inner gesture, the sages call it ratzon: will, not from lack but from overflowing. The Midrash on Genesis 1:1 and passages in Zohar 1:15a speak of this overflowing as the desire to be known.

From this will, a first tilt arises. The Infinite does not move from here to there; rather, it allows a tiny asymmetry, a place for a world to appear. In the language of prayer, this is the heart preparing to say the Shema of Berakhot 2a. In the language of physics, it is the moment a symmetric field quietly breaks symmetry. That almost-imperceptible leaning is the work of Aleph.
