Sefer Aleph — Etz HaReishit (The Tree of Origin)

Preface — Before the Tree

A short excerpt from the opening of Sefer Aleph. You can paste the full preface text here as it grows.

In the beginning of this book, there is no tree. No branches, no leaves glistening with dew, no roots tunneling into dark soil. There is only One: הָאֵין סוֹף, the endless, the field before every field. Modern physics stumbles toward this in its own language, speaking of unified fields and vacuum states. Kabbalah speaks of Ein Sof, a life so whole that “inside” and “outside” have not yet been imagined.

This Sefer is not a physics textbook and not a technical kabbalistic manual. It is a guided walk through the hunch that both are circling: that reality is one living Field which knows how to appear as many.

Chapter Aleph — The First Shimmer of Existence

Aleph as the first almost-sound: the space that lets words be spoken. The chapter unfolds Aleph in three faces.

Aleph as Field

Aleph as the white fire behind the letters: Ein Sof as an unbroken, ever-present field in which all phenomena arise. In parallel, the ground state of quantum fields and the “vacuum” that is never truly empty.

Aleph as Tilt

The first inclination within the Field: tzimtzum as a contemplative picture of symmetry breaking, a subtle leaning that allows distinction without severance.

Aleph as Bridge

The written form of Aleph as upper point, lower point, and diagonal: keter and malchut in conversation, direct and returning light, the way the Infinite and the finite speak through each other in every moment.

Excerpt from Chapter Aleph

Imagine absolute darkness—not the darkness of a room where a switch could be flipped, but a darkness in which “light” and “dark” have never yet been conceived. No up, no down, no before or after. Only a fullness so complete that there is no edge to mark it. If such a fullness could have an inner gesture, the sages call it ratzon: will, not from lack but from overflowing.

From this will, a first tilt arises. The Infinite does not move from here to there; rather, it allows a tiny asymmetry, a place for a world to appear. That almost-imperceptible leaning is the work of Aleph.
