Etz HaMar’ah — The Tree of Mirrors

A Tree grown from reflection: left and right, inside and outside, Yetzer HaRa and Yetzer HaTov. In the Mirror Tree, the “harsh” left side of the Etz Chaim reveals itself as hidden Chesed when seen through the glass.

This page gathers the vision, themes, and structure for the Tree of Mirrors. As the Sefer and/or novel takes shape, it can link to sample chapters, PDFs, and study guides.

מְרְאָה

What looks like Gevurah may be Chesed in reflection.

The Tree that lives in the glass

Etz HaMar’ah began with a simple insight: if you stand before a mirror and raise your left hand, the image raises its right. The “harsh” left side of the Etz Chaim may, in reflection, be a secret form of kindness. Judgment becomes protection, boundaries become shelter, and what we call darkness sometimes turns out to be mercy seen from the wrong side of the glass.

This Tree is a meditation on mirrors: bathroom mirrors, shop windows at night, black phone screens, and the invisible inward mirrors where we rehearse every shame. It is also a study of how a life looks from the inside versus how it looks to others.

In the Quantum Etz Chaim frame, Etz HaMar’ah explores what happens when the one Field “copies” itself with a slight inversion. Every gate, every branch, has a mirrored counterpart. Some are distorted (like carnival mirrors). Some are clarifying. The work of this Sefer is to tell stories and offer frameworks where the mirror becomes not a trap, but a tool for teshuvah.

What lives in the Tree of Mirrors

Core themes and questions that run through Etz HaMar’ah.

Left and right

The halachic and kabbalistic “left side” (Gevurah, Din) versus the “right side” (Chesed). In the mirror, they swap. What does that mean for how we see discipline, boundaries, and the left-handed among us?

Yetzer HaRa & Yetzer HaTov

A core idea: the same impulse that can wreck a life in one direction can build a world in another. The Mirror Tree wants to show how redirected desire becomes mitzvah, not by erasing the past but by reflecting it.

Mirrors & mental health

How we see ourselves is often harsher than how HaShem sees us. The Tree of Mirrors listens to those inner reflections—anxiety, shame, intrusive thoughts—and slowly introduces another view: “This, too, can be reflected into kindness.”

Etz HaMareh & Etz HaChaim

In some scenes, the Tree of Mirrors is literally a reflected Etz Chaim: the left-hand column now appears on the right. In others, it is a shattered mirror that must be reassembled, piece by glittering piece.

Family and memory

This Tree can quietly honor left-handed children, outsiders, and anyone who grew up feeling “the wrong way around.” Reflection becomes a way to bless what once felt like a flaw.

Physics of reflection

On the quantum side: symmetry, parity, and what happens when the laws of physics are mirrored. The Sefer can use these ideas gently—as metaphors that hint at deeper symmetries in the soul.

Sample from the opening

A short taste of the voice and atmosphere of Etz HaMar’ah. This can be swapped out or expanded as chapters grow.

The mirror over the sink had never liked him.

It was not that it distorted—on the contrary, it was too honest. In the flat, indifferent glass he met a man whose left hand had built its own religion of reaching for the wrong things. The right hand held the toothbrush; the left remembered every bottle, every door he should not have opened, every face that had watched him slip away.

Most mornings he could ignore it. Splash of water, eyes half shut, the world still mercifully blurred. But there were days when the mirror seemed to lean closer, as though the silver backing had thinned and something on the other side was trying, gently, to get his attention.

What if, the not-quite-voice asked, you are not seeing yourself at all? What if you have only ever seen a reversal?

He raised his left hand experimentally. The man in the mirror raised his right, and somewhere deep in the architecture of the Tree, a column that had always been called “severity” shifted, just a little, toward mercy.

Structure & progress

A living outline for how the Tree of Mirrors might be written.

Form: Sefer, novel, or both

Etz HaMar’ah can exist as a Sefer (short chapters, reflective essays, sources and exercises) or as a novel (following one protagonist through mirrored lives)—or as a hybrid that alternates between the two.

Chapters as mirrors

Each chapter can mirror another: what happens “on the left” in one chapter is seen “from the right” in another. The table of contents itself can be laid out as paired reflections.

Sources & learning

Verses about eyes, faces, and hearts; passages on Yetzer HaRa and HaTov; kabbalistic texts on the pillars of the Etz Chaim. All of these can sit in notes or sidebars, pointing learners back to the primary texts.

Integration with the 231 Gates

Specific Gates—like אד (Aleph–Dalet), זג (Zayin–Gimel), or pairs involving letters of seeing (ע) and hearing (ע / א)— can be flagged as “mirror-gates” that belong especially to Etz HaMar’ah.

Current status

Concept, themes, and sample voice established here. The next steps are to choose whether to draft as Sefer, novel, or braided form, and to sketch a ringed chapter outline like the Stone Tree’s rings.
